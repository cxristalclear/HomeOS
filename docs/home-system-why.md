# The Home System — Why

*Read this when scope or motivation drifts. If a feature doesn't serve the bottleneck, it doesn't belong.*

## The bottleneck

The optimizer — knowing what's due, when, and in what order — lives only in my head. On a bad week it goes dark with no backup, the house loses steering, and it can't restart itself (because restarting also needs me, and I'm the one who's down).

The system exists to move the optimizer **out of my head** into something that runs without me.

## Why it's not a willpower problem

She's the **executor** — give her a defined job and she does it. I'm the **optimizer** — I run the planning nobody can see. Different functions, not unequal effort. The catch: the optimizer and the person who fails under load are the same person, and on a bad week we *both* go down. So the optimizer can't live in either of us. It has to live in the system.

## How it shows up

Dishes pile (most-visible handoff, so it breaks first), laundry stalls at the put-away handoff, weekends become resets, weeknights get spent cleaning, "few times a week" tasks collapse into the weekend. Five+ chore systems tried and abandoned.

## The rules the build must follow

1. **The system decides what's due — not me.** If a human has to plan it, we've rebuilt the thing that breaks.
2. **No debt, ever.** No streaks, no "behind" guilt. Overdue just floats up. Re-entry = do today's top thing, nothing owed.
3. **Cadence re-anchors to when I actually did it**, not the calendar. Target is a goal, not a debt generator.
4. **It tells *her* the job — not me.** No middleman, no nagging.
5. **Zero upkeep of the system itself.** Tap done, it resets. No charts to feed.

## The test for any feature

> Does this help on the **worst** week, or only a good one?

If only a good week, cut it. Good weeks were never the problem.
